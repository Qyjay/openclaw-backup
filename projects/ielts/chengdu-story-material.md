## Part 1: The Unified Story (完整故事线)

### The Chengdu Trip — Master Narrative

Last summer, my good friend David and I took a five-day trip to Chengdu, and honestly, it turned out to be one of the best experiences I've ever had.

The whole thing started months before we actually went. I'd been stuck between Chengdu, Chongqing, and Yunnan for ages — couldn't make up my mind at all. David sat me down one evening, pulled up a spreadsheet — yeah, a literal spreadsheet — and compared the three cities: cost, weather, attractions, food scene, everything. He said Chengdu had the best balance for a first-time visit. I trusted him, and looking back, that was absolutely the right call.

Once we locked in Chengdu, David went full planning mode. He made this incredibly detailed itinerary — hour by hour, which restaurant to hit, which route to take between attractions, even backup options in case of rain. He color-coded it. I'm not even exaggerating. Every night during the trip, he'd sit on his hotel bed writing in his travel diary, organizing the day's photos into folders. It was almost obsessive, but in the best way. I remember thinking, "I really need to pick up this habit."

We flew into Chengdu and spent the first morning at the Chengdu Research Base of Giant Panda Breeding. That was genuinely magical. We got there early — around eight — and the pandas were having breakfast, just munching on bamboo, tumbling around, completely unbothered by the crowd. Everyone around us was grinning from ear to ear, including me and David. There was this one baby panda that kept rolling off a log, and the whole crowd burst out laughing every time. A volunteer guide there — a middle-aged woman, super passionate — gave us a mini-lecture about panda conservation. She talked about how their habitat is shrinking, how the breeding programs work, and she encouraged everyone to cut down on single-use plastics. That really stuck with me.

While we were at the Panda Base, a couple of foreign tourists — American, I think — came up to us looking a bit lost. They were trying to find the nursery area. David, without hesitation, walked over and helped them with directions. For me, it was actually the first time I'd ever had a real conversation in English outside of a classroom. My heart was pounding, but I managed to explain the route, and they thanked me and said my English was good. I was buzzing for the rest of the day.

On day two, we rented a car and drove out to Dujiangyan, the ancient irrigation system. The drive itself was gorgeous — lush green mountains, winding roads, mist hanging over the valleys. David was navigating while I drove, and we kept stopping to take photos. Dujiangyan was incredible — this 2,000-year-old engineering marvel surrounded by thick forest and the sound of rushing water. It felt like stepping back in time.

That evening, we visited Wuhou Shrine, dedicated to Zhuge Liang and Liu Bei from the Three Kingdoms period. Our audio guide told us the story of Liu Bei visiting Zhuge Liang's thatched cottage three times to recruit him — the famous "Three Visits to the Thatched Cottage." The gardens there were stunning — ancient cypress trees, quiet paths, red walls with bamboo shadows. It felt like a natural sanctuary right in the middle of the city. We also stopped at the IFS mall to see the giant panda sculpture climbing up the building — such an iconic Chengdu landmark. And then we wandered through Taikoo Li, this open-air shopping complex where luxury boutiques sit right next to traditional Qing-dynasty style architecture. The contrast was wild.

Day three brought our most chaotic moment. We were exploring the old alleys near Kuanzhai Alley — narrow lanes, crumbling walls, locals playing mahjong outside — and we completely lost our way. No phone signal, GPS spinning in circles. We had to swallow our pride and ask an old lady sitting outside her door. She didn't speak any Mandarin — pure Sichuan dialect — but with a lot of pointing and laughing, she got us back on track.

That same evening, we had the hotpot dinner that I still dream about. A proper Chengdu hotpot place, packed and noisy and absolutely buzzing. We ordered maodu, duck intestine, huanghou — the works. David was terrified of spicy food. He kept saying, "I can't do this, I really can't." I told him, "Come on, you've come all the way to Chengdu — you can't NOT try the real thing." He finally dipped a piece of maodu in the red oil, chewed it, and his face went through about five emotions in two seconds. But then he grinned and said, "Okay, that's actually incredible." We ended up staying for two hours, sweating and laughing and ordering more.

On day four, we stumbled upon this old tea shop hidden in a narrow alley, run by a husband-and-wife team who'd been there for over thirty years. They were so warm — the wife brewed us sample after sample, telling us about each variety, where the leaves came from, how they were processed. I only planned to buy one small bag, but their passion was so infectious that I walked out with four bags and a teapot. Spent way more than I'd budgeted, but honestly, no regrets.

That night, we headed to Jiuyanqiao bar street. We'd heard it was the nightlife hub, so we expected some chill live music. Instead, it was blasting EDM, strobe lights, way too crowded, and the bass was so loud I could feel it in my chest. We lasted about forty minutes before we looked at each other and just walked out. Not our scene at all.

Throughout the entire trip, we relied on Meituan for basically everything — finding restaurants, booking the hotel, ordering late-night takeout, even unlocking shared bikes. It was our lifeline.

One stressful moment: our hotel booking got canceled last minute on the third night — some system error. David didn't panic at all. He pulled out his phone, found three alternatives within ten minutes, compared prices, checked reviews, and booked a better place for less money. I would've just stood there stressing, but David treated it like a puzzle to solve.

He was like that the whole trip. He helped a stranger carry luggage up a staircase at the train station, gave directions to lost tourists, and even helped me find my phone when I left it in a restaurant. David's just genuinely kind — the type who helps without being asked.

By the last day, I didn't want to leave. There was still so much we hadn't explored. We promised each other we'd come back — maybe in winter, to see the pandas in the snow. That trip didn't just show me Chengdu; it showed me what David was really like as a person, and it made our friendship so much deeper.

### L4: 自然之地

**Part 2 Answer:**

The natural place I want to describe is actually Dujiangyan, which I visited during my Chengdu trip last summer with David. It's about an hour's drive from central Chengdu, and it completely blew me away.

Dujiangyan is famous as an ancient irrigation system — it's over 2,000 years old — but what really struck me was the natural environment surrounding it. The whole area is nestled in these lush green mountains, with dense forest on both sides and the Min River rushing through the middle. ### O17: 学习朋友的好习惯

**话题**: Describe a good habit you learned from a friend  
**切入角度**: 以 David 每晚写旅行日记、整理照片、做计划的习惯为核心。

**Part 2 Answer:**

The habit I learned from my friend David is keeping a regular record of things — like a journal or diary. I'd never really done this before our Chengdu trip last summer, but watching David do it every single night really inspired me.

So every evening during our trip, after a full day of exploring, I'd be lying on the bed scrolling through my phone, completely wiped out. And David would be sitting at the desk, writing in his travel diary — describing what we did, what he felt, funny moments, even what we ate. Then he'd organize the day's photos into folders by location and delete the blurry ones. This whole routine took him maybe thirty minutes, and he did it every single night without fail.

At first, I thought it was a bit excessive. Like, "Dude, just relax, we're on vacation." But when we got back home and I tried to remember the details of each day, everything was already blurring together. Meanwhile, David had this beautifully organized record — he could tell you exactly what we ate on day three, which alley we got lost in, what the volunteer at the Panda Base said. His memories were preserved in sharp detail while mine were already fading.

That really hit me. I realized that experiences, no matter how vivid in the moment, fade faster than you think. Since that trip, I've started keeping a weekly journal — nothing as detailed as David's, just a few bullet points about what happened and how I felt. It's already become one of my favorite habits. Sometimes I read back through old entries and discover things I'd completely forgotten.

David didn't tell me to start journaling. He just did it consistently, and I saw the value. I think the best way to pick up a good habit is watching someone you respect live it naturally.
