## Topic 1: 擅长做计划的人

**Describe a person who makes plans a lot and is good at planning**

You should say: Who he or she is / How you knew him or her / What plans he or she makes / And explain how you feel about this person

**Answer:**

> Well, the person I'd like to talk about is my close friend Leo, who I met during my first year at Nankai University. We were in the same dormitory building and ended up taking the same elective course, which is how we got to know each other. From the very beginning, I noticed something quite distinctive about him — he's incredibly organised, almost to the point where it's a personality trait rather than just a habit.
>
> What really sets Leo apart is his Sunday evening ritual. Every single week, without fail, he sits down and maps out his entire week — classes, study blocks, gym sessions, even when he's going to do laundry. He colour-codes everything in this notebook he carries around, and honestly, the first time I saw it, I thought he was a bit intense. But the thing is, it actually works. During exam season, while the rest of us are scrambling at the last minute, Leo's already reviewed everything according to his schedule. He also plans trips in incredible detail — last year when a group of us went to Nanjing, he'd already sorted out transport, accommodation, and a day-by-day itinerary before anyone else had even packed.

> Going forward, I genuinely think this habit is going to serve him brilliantly in whatever career he pursues, because that level of discipline is rare in people our age. I've actually started picking up some of his methods myself — nothing as elaborate, but I now write a simple to-do list every morning.
>
> Ultimately, what I admire most about Leo isn't just the planning itself — it's the calm confidence it gives him. He never seems stressed or caught off guard, and being around someone like that is honestly reassuring. He's taught me that a bit of structure can go a very long way.

### Part 3

**Q1: Do you think it's important to plan ahead?**

> On balance, I'd say it's quite important, particularly when there's a lot at stake. Planning ahead gives people a sense of direction and helps them allocate their time more effectively — take exam revision, for instance, where having a structured timetable can genuinely make the difference between passing and failing. That said, I think there's a fine line between healthy planning and over-planning. If someone becomes so rigid that they can't adapt when circumstances change, the plan itself becomes a burden. So I'd argue it's about finding the right balance between preparation and flexibility.

**Q2: What activities do we need to plan ahead?**

> I'd say any activity that involves multiple people, significant costs, or tight deadlines really benefits from advance planning. Travel is the most obvious example — you'd want to sort out flights, accommodation, and visas well before the departure date, or you risk paying through the nose. Similarly, academic projects and work presentations need forethought, because leaving things to the last minute usually shows in the quality. On the flip side, everyday decisions like what to eat for lunch probably don't warrant much planning. So it really comes down to the complexity and consequences of the activity.

**Q3: Do you think children should plan their future career?**

> Hmm, that's an interesting one. I'm inclined to think that children should be encouraged to explore different interests rather than locking themselves into a specific career path too early. At that age, their understanding of the working world is quite limited, so any plan they make would likely change drastically anyway. A case in point — I wanted to be an astronaut when I was eight, and now I'm studying software engineering. That said, having a general sense of direction can be motivating. It's the difference between dreaming and being forced into a rigid five-year plan, and I think the former is healthy while the latter is not.

**Q4: Should children ask their teachers or parents for advice when making plans?**

> From my perspective, both can offer valuable but quite different insights. Parents tend to understand a child's personality, emotional needs, and family circumstances, so their advice often comes from a place of deep personal knowledge. Teachers, on the other hand, have a broader view of academic pathways and professional development — they've seen many students go through similar decisions. Ideally, children should consult both, because the advice complements rather than competes. What I'd caution against is relying on just one source, since that can create blind spots in the decision-making process.

### Part 3

**Q1: What is the right age for a child to learn drawing?**

> Honestly, I don't think there's a single "right" age — children naturally start scribbling and experimenting with colours from about three or four, and that early play is actually an important form of creative development. The key, I think, is that formal instruction shouldn't come too early. If you push structured drawing lessons on a four-year-old, you risk sucking the joy out of it. Let them explore freely first, and then perhaps around seven or eight, if they show genuine interest, introduce some techniques. Ultimately, the goal should be nurturing creativity, not producing mini-Picassos.

**Q2: Why do most children draw more often than adults do?**

> The way I see it, children draw more because they haven't yet developed the self-consciousness that holds adults back. A child will happily draw a purple elephant and feel great about it, whereas most adults worry about whether their work looks "good enough." On top of that, children have significantly more free time and fewer responsibilities — drawing is one of the most accessible forms of play. Adults, by contrast, are caught up in work and household duties, and when they do find spare time, they tend to gravitate towards passive activities like scrolling social media rather than picking up a pencil.

**Q3: Why do some people visit galleries or museums instead of viewing artworks online?**

> I'd argue it comes down to the sensory experience. Seeing a painting in person — the texture of the brushstrokes, the actual scale of the canvas, the way light plays across the surface — is fundamentally different from viewing a flat image on a screen. Museums also create a particular atmosphere of contemplation that your living room simply can't replicate. Mind you, online viewing has its advantages, especially for accessibility, but it's a bit like comparing a live concert to listening through earphones. Both have merit, but the visceral impact of being physically present is hard to match.

**Q4: Do you think galleries and museums should be free of charge?**

> To a large extent, yes, especially for public or national collections. Art and culture should be accessible to everyone, not just those who can afford a ticket. When museums are free, you see a much more diverse range of visitors, including students and lower-income families, which enriches the whole experience. That said, I recognise that museums need revenue to maintain their collections and pay staff. A compromise that works well is offering free general admission while charging for special exhibitions. That way, you preserve access without starving the institution of funds.

### Part 3

**Q1: What are the advantages and disadvantages of being a famous child?**

> Well, on the plus side, famous children often get access to extraordinary opportunities — better education, wider networks, and a head start in their chosen field. They might also develop confidence earlier from public exposure. However, the flip side is quite troubling. Famous children lose their privacy during the most formative years of their lives, and the pressure to perform can be psychologically damaging. Take child actors, for instance — many have spoken publicly about how fame robbed them of a normal childhood. So while the material advantages are obvious, the emotional costs shouldn't be underestimated.

**Q2: What can today's children do to become famous?**

> These days, the most common route is through social media platforms. A child with a particular talent — dancing, gaming, comedy — can upload content and potentially reach millions of viewers overnight. That's fundamentally different from a generation ago, when fame typically required industry gatekeepers like record labels or TV networks. Admittedly, this democratisation of fame has its risks: children may prioritise online validation over education, and they're exposed to public scrutiny at an incredibly vulnerable age. So while the pathways exist, I'd argue that parents need to be very involved in guiding the process.

**Q3: What can children do with their fame?**

> If managed wisely, famous children can use their platform to raise awareness about important issues — anti-bullying campaigns, environmental protection, or simply inspiring peers to pursue their passions. Some young influencers have raised significant funds for charity, which is remarkable. Having said that, children are not equipped to handle the commercial and reputational complexities of fame on their own. They need adult supervision to ensure their platform is used constructively rather than exploitatively. The potential is enormous, but so are the risks.

**Q4: Do people become famous because of their talent?**

> Talent certainly opens doors, but I'd say it's far from the only factor. Timing, connections, and increasingly, one's ability to self-promote on social media all play significant roles. You can find extraordinarily talented musicians busking on street corners who'll never be famous, while some people with moderate abilities but excellent marketing become household names. A case in point is the rise of viral internet celebrities — their fame often stems from a single moment of relatability rather than years of honed skill. So talent matters, but the ecosystem around it matters just as much.

### Part 3

**Q1: How can parents teach their children to protect nature?**

> I think the most effective approach is leading by example rather than lecturing. When children see their parents recycling, conserving water, or choosing reusable bags, those behaviours become normalised. Beyond that, giving children direct contact with nature — hiking, gardening, visiting national parks — builds an emotional connection that no classroom lesson can replicate. My own parents, for example, took me back to the mountains of Enshi every summer, and that firsthand experience of pristine nature made me care about preserving it far more than any textbook chapter would have.

**Q2: Should schools teach children to get close to nature?**

> Absolutely. In an era where children spend increasingly long hours staring at screens, structured time in nature is arguably more important than ever. Schools are uniquely positioned to facilitate this, whether through outdoor science lessons, school gardens, or field trips. The benefits extend beyond environmental awareness — being outdoors has been shown to reduce stress, improve concentration, and boost physical health. To be fair, urban schools face logistical challenges in arranging regular outdoor activities, but even small efforts like maintaining a classroom plant or visiting a local park can make a meaningful difference.

**Q3: Do you think there should be laws to protect nature?**

> Without question. While individual responsibility matters, voluntary action alone is insufficient to address environmental degradation on any meaningful scale. Laws set enforceable standards — emissions limits, deforestation penalties, wildlife protection regulations — that apply equally to individuals and corporations. Without legal frameworks, there's nothing stopping companies from dumping waste into rivers or logging protected forests if it's profitable. That said, laws are only as effective as their enforcement. Passing environmental legislation is one thing; ensuring compliance is another challenge entirely, particularly in developing regions.

### Part 3

**Q1: Do you think children are born smart or they learn to become smart?**

> I'm inclined to think it's predominantly the latter, though genetics certainly plays a role. Some children may have a natural aptitude for pattern recognition or language, but without stimulation, practice, and quality education, that potential often goes unrealised. Conversely, a child who receives consistent encouragement and intellectual challenge can develop remarkable abilities regardless of their starting point. The research on neuroplasticity supports this — the brain continues to form new connections throughout life in response to learning. So while nature provides the raw material, nurture is what shapes intelligence.

**Q2: How do children become smart at school?**

> It's a combination of factors working together. Active engagement is crucial — children who ask questions, participate in discussions, and challenge ideas tend to develop stronger critical thinking skills than those who passively absorb information. Good teaching obviously matters too; a teacher who explains concepts through real-world examples and encourages curiosity makes a profound difference. Beyond the classroom, habits like consistent reading, reflecting on mistakes, and collaborating with peers all contribute. I'd also highlight the role of a growth mindset — children who believe intelligence is something they can develop, rather than a fixed trait, tend to push themselves further.

**Q3: Why are some people well-rounded and others only good at one thing?**

> I think it comes down to a mix of curiosity, opportunity, and personal choice. Some people are naturally drawn to exploring many different fields — they read widely, try various hobbies, and enjoy the novelty of learning something new. Others find one area that truly captivates them and pour all their energy into mastering it. Neither approach is inherently better; a well-rounded person brings breadth, while a specialist brings depth. That said, upbringing and education systems also play a role. Schools that expose children to diverse subjects and activities tend to produce more well-rounded individuals, whereas highly competitive or specialised training programs foster expertise in a single domain.

**Q4: Why does modern society need talents of all kinds?**

> Put simply, the challenges we face today are too complex and interconnected for any single type of expertise to address alone. Developing an AI product, for instance — which I've seen firsthand in my internship — requires not just programmers, but designers, ethicists, marketers, and linguists all working in concert. A society that only values one type of talent would be deeply dysfunctional. Arguably, the greatest innovations emerge at the intersection of different disciplines — where an artist's creativity meets an engineer's precision, or where a psychologist's insight informs a tech company's product design. Diversity of talent isn't a luxury; it's a necessity.

### Part 3

**Q1: Should children help their parents with household chores?**

> I'd say yes, with a caveat — the chores should be age-appropriate and framed as shared responsibility rather than punishment. When children help around the house, they develop practical skills and an understanding that family life requires cooperation. Something as simple as setting the table or tidying their own room instils a sense of accountability. However, if parents overload children with chores to the point where it cuts into study time or play, that's counterproductive. The goal is building character, not creating miniature domestic workers.

**Q2: What kind of help do people need when looking for a new job?**

> The needs tend to fall into three categories: informational, practical, and emotional. On the information side, people need guidance on which industries are hiring, what skills are in demand, and how to identify suitable roles. Practically, they often need help polishing their CV, preparing for interviews, and sometimes acquiring new qualifications. But the emotional dimension is frequently overlooked — job hunting can be deeply stressful and demoralising, especially after repeated rejections. Having someone who believes in you and provides honest encouragement can be just as valuable as any technical advice.

**Q3: Who should people ask for help, colleagues or family members?**

> It really depends on the nature of the problem. For work-specific challenges — say, navigating office politics or handling a technical issue — colleagues are generally more helpful because they understand the context. Family members, however, are better equipped to offer emotional support and long-term perspective, because they know you as a whole person, not just a job title. The ideal scenario is having both available: colleagues for professional guidance and family for the emotional bedrock. What I'd avoid is expecting one group to fill the other's role, because that often leads to frustration on both sides.

### Part 3

**Q1: Should people prepare before giving advice?**

> Definitely. Giving advice is a form of responsibility — if you speak without understanding the full picture, you might do more harm than good. Preparation doesn't mean writing a script; it means listening carefully, considering the other person's situation, and thinking about potential consequences. I've seen well-intentioned advice backfire spectacularly because the adviser was projecting their own experience onto someone else's problem. So yes, taking a moment to reflect before speaking is not just helpful — it's respectful.

**Q2: Is it good to ask advice from strangers online?**

> It can be, within limits. Online communities often bring together people with diverse experiences, so you might find perspectives that friends and family simply can't offer. Anonymity can also make it easier to discuss sensitive topics without fear of judgment. However, the quality of online advice is wildly inconsistent — you're just as likely to encounter uninformed opinions as genuine expertise. Not to mention privacy risks: sharing personal problems on public forums can have unintended consequences. So I'd treat online advice as one input among many, never the sole basis for a decision.

**Q3: What are the personalities of people whose job is to give advice to others?**

> People in advisory roles — therapists, counsellors, consultants — tend to share certain traits. Patience is paramount, because understanding someone's problem requires careful, sometimes lengthy listening. Empathy is equally important; you can't give meaningful advice if you can't see the world through the other person's eyes. Beyond that, I'd add intellectual honesty — a good adviser admits when they don't have the answer rather than bluffing. And finally, emotional stability: if you're easily rattled by other people's problems, you won't be effective in helping them navigate those problems calmly.

**Q4: What are the problems if you ask too many people for advice?**

> The biggest issue is information overload leading to paralysis. When you collect ten different opinions, they're almost guaranteed to conflict, and instead of feeling more confident, you end up more confused than when you started. There's also the risk of losing your own voice — constantly seeking external validation can erode your ability to trust your own judgment. On a social level, it can strain relationships too, because people who give you advice often expect you to follow it, and you obviously can't follow ten contradictory suggestions simultaneously. So my rule of thumb is: consult two or three trusted people, then make your own call.
